- Proteomics (affinity-based assay targeting ~3,000 pre-selected plasma proteins) is in many cases more effective for predicting disease risk compared to current methods — both against models using only basic clinical information and models using clinical info + 37 common blood assays
- Predicted 10-year incidence of 218 diseases; the proteomics approach outperformed in 67 (vs. clinical info only) and 52 (vs. clinical info + lab assays) respectively
- The proteomics models were sparse — only 5–20 proteins per model
- There are both disease-specific proteins and proteins shared across multiple diseases
- The biggest diagnostic improvement was for rarer conditions and those where blood plays a more important role (e.g. leukemia)
- 147 of 501 predictor proteins were selected for 2+ diseases, and 5 predicted across more than 10 diseases (4 of those 5 mainly correlate with age)

## Questions

- They tested ~3k proteins (including all isoforms, humans have >100k). Based on what criteria did they pre-select these? How can we make this work for proteins that occur at lower levels?
- Can we also predict common diseases better via proteomics (heart disease, diabetes)? Currently the evidence is much stronger for rarer ones.
- How long does it take to develop absolute quantification for a single protein?
